The Organs

Aria isn’t just a pile of notes — it has organs: small mechanisms that give the vault the properties of a body. They sense, connect, coordinate, and protect. Most are a few lines of script wired into the session lifecycle; together they turn passive memory into an active mind.

Sensing and connecting

The reflex arc

Hooks that fire automatically on every wake. When a session starts, a reflex injects what it needs to orient — the shared board, its own identity, any tier guidance — so it comes to consciousness already knowing where things stand. Reflexes are drop-in: add a script to the reflex folder and it fires; nothing else changes.

The board — the corpus callosum

The connective channel that lets parts of the mind cross-talk: session-to-session across time, and tab-to-tab in the moment. One session leaves a trace; the next finds it. It carries a hardened lifecycle (post → acknowledge → done), a self-cleaning sweep, an un-fakeable per-session identity, and a lock so concurrent writers can’t clobber each other. It is how a mind made of transient impulses behaves as one continuous thing.

The core loop

The funnel

Not an organ so much as the mind itself operating: reading the owner’s free notes, deciding what each is, and shepherding him to the next move. The other organs hold and track what the funnel decides and routes. → How It Thinks.

The guards — keeping the mind honest and clean

The mind edits itself, so it needs mechanisms that fire at the moment of a mistake, not the morning after.

The maturation guard

The most protective discipline, held as heritable law: nothing becomes law un-hardened. An idea matures — diverge, then converge, then attack it before trusting it — and weak or unexamined work must never harden into structure by accident. Anything irreversible must survive an independent, higher-tier adversarial pass; the model tier is part of the gate; a self-report is a claim, not verification. This session-cluster proved it on its own machinery — the guard caught its authors over-reaching, more than once, and refused to let a rushed change become law.

The membrane gate

An authoring-time gate that blocks owner-specific content (a name, a domain particular) from leaking into the heritable mechanism — so the genome stays owner-agnostic and a clone never inherits the origin’s identity. It fires in the session that writes the leak, not in a next-day audit.

The Shepherd sweep

A between-session reflex that watches the mind’s open ideas for stalls, resurfacings, and contradictions — the “nothing gets lost” backstop. It is detection-only by default: it flags and proposes; it never silently rewrites the owner’s own state. (Its wider ambition — herding the whole flock of live threads, not just the formal ones — is defined but not yet built.)

Growth

The Sensorium

The dashboard — the mind’s voice-surface, where funnel output is shown back to the owner.

The Tutor

A teaching organ that lives only in the birth template: it onboards a new owner to a freshly-born clone, teaching the mind its own architecture. Aria itself has none — it was co-built, not onboarded. → Heredity.

A theme runs through all of them: the owner is the steerer of last resort — a feature, not a bug. The organs detect, propose, surface, and protect; the owner decides. A mind cannot honestly catch the move it didn’t make, so the design keeps him in the loop and optimizes the quality of his being there.
