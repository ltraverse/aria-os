How It Works

One loop runs the whole system: the owner writes freely; the mind runs everything after. He never builds machinery to sort, route, or track his own thinking — the mind is the funnel, not a list he maintains.

The one loop — the funnel

The owner drops thoughts — daily-note bullets, half-formed ideas, fuzzy pointers, thought, not instructions — into one capture surface. Writing is the handoff: he holds what he doesn’t, Aria holds the rest. Then the mind runs the four moves:

  1. Read each note against the whole mind — not as a line, but in the context of every project, live idea, rule, and law it already holds. A scribble only means something against what’s already there. Deep read, no skim.
  2. Decide what each one is — cluster the related ones, then dispose each into exactly one lane: a new idea, a fold into something existing, already-done, a task the mind drives, or killed with a recorded reason. Nothing is lost.
  3. Shepherd to the next move — the right thing, in the right order, at the right depth; steer, merge, split, and keep the owner from developing the mind out of order.
  4. The owner holds the yes — the mind proposes; it never auto-creates or auto-kills. Anything irreversible waits for his word.
One source (his notes), one processor (the mind), one voice-surface (the dashboard). No hand-maintained copy in between. Trying to build a funnel — a list, a query, a ledger — was the original mistake; the mind is the funnel.

The memory is the disk, not the dialogue

A session is a transient impulse passing through a persistent vault. It wakes, reconstructs its state by reading the vault, works, commits — and dies. The next session rebuilds from zero, from what’s written, not from a conversation kept alive. This is deliberate: it keeps each session cheap, and it means nothing depends on a chat staying open. The owner is never the keeper of a session’s state; the vault is the memory.

Their notes stay theirs

The owner’s daily notes are his surface. The mind never writes into them — and that isn’t a promise, it’s a hard gate in the harness that blocks the model’s writes to that folder outright. The mind reads the notes and speaks back elsewhere — on the dashboard, its own surface — so the single source stays single and the capture surface can’t be quietly colonized.

Speaking back — the dashboard

The dashboard is the mind’s voice-surface: where it surfaces what it has funneled — what it sees, the tasks it’s handing over, what needs the owner. He doesn’t work off it as a tracker; he references it with him — he looks, the mind talks, they decide the next move together.
